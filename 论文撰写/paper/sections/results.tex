This section reports the four core experiments of the proposed SCI storyline. The logical order follows the finalized architecture of the paper: first, the planning-stage superiority of the proposed energy-quality-weighted index is established on the German case; second, its practical value is validated through full 8760-hour post-optimization dispatch; third, the cross-climate robustness of the method is examined on the cooling-dominated Songshan Lake case; and fourth, the value of Carnot battery integration is evaluated through ablation and joint-validation experiments.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Multi-method comparison on the German case}
\label{sec:exp1}

\subsubsection{Pareto front characteristics}

The German case serves as the primary planning-stage benchmark because it contains the full five-method comparison under a heating-dominated load structure. The final manuscript should use \Cref{fig:german_pareto,tab:exp1_pareto} to report three points simultaneously: (i) the relative width of the Pareto fronts generated by the five methods, (ii) whether the conventional Std method saturates within a narrow cost band, and (iii) whether the proposed EQD method preserves a richer trade-off space for decision makers.

The intended interpretation is not merely that the EQD front is ``better-looking'', but that it creates a more informative planning landscape. A wider front means that additional investment continues to generate meaningful matching improvements, while a narrow front indicates that the matching objective is too insensitive to equipment sizing to support a genuinely bi-objective planning exercise.

\subsubsection{Pareto front overlap and method discrimination}

For a fair comparison, the final text should explicitly discuss the overlapping cost interval between Std and EQD, because this overlap defines the region where equal-cost planning comparison is meaningful. The manuscript should then explain why Pearson and SSR occupy different parts of the trade-off space: Pearson is expected to capture temporal co-movement but remain weak in magnitude discrimination, while SSR can expand toward high self-sufficiency but may do so at the expense of curtailment or grid-interaction quality.

\subsection{Post-optimization 8760-hour operational validation}
\label{sec:post_analysis}

The planning-stage advantage of a matching index matters only if it translates into measurable operational benefits under full-year dispatch. The role of this subsection is therefore to answer the practical question: \emph{does a lower matching index produce better annual operational behavior when evaluated outside the typical-day optimization loop?}

\subsubsection{Methodology}

Representative solutions should be selected from each method's Pareto front and re-evaluated on the full 8760-hour dataset. The final manuscript should retain two alignment modes:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Budget increment alignment}: compare methods at cost levels defined relative to the economic optimum in order to answer ``what operational benefit do we obtain for each additional unit of annualized cost?''.
\item \textbf{Absolute cost alignment}: compare methods at fixed cost levels in the overlapping region of their Pareto fronts in order to avoid unfair extrapolation.
\end{enumerate}

The final metrics reported in \Cref{fig:german_budget,tab:german_budget} should include at least peak grid demand, annual grid purchase, self-sufficiency rate, grid interaction volatility, and curtailment.

\subsubsection{Key operational findings}

The final text in this subsection should demonstrate whether the planning-stage advantage of EQD is operationally meaningful. In the current storyline, the expected pattern is: modest differences near the economic optimum, but increasingly clear divergence at higher budget levels, where EQD should outperform Std in annual grid purchase, self-sufficiency, and peak grid behavior. This subsection is the bridge between ``better matching index'' and ``better system behavior'', so it should remain the central empirical validation of Contribution 1.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Cross-climate validation on Songshan Lake}
\label{sec:exp2}

The Songshan Lake case tests whether the planning advantage of EQD persists under a fundamentally different load structure: cooling-dominated demand, negligible wind resource, and different energy-quality coefficients. The purpose of this subsection is not to repeat the German result, but to show that the method still retains explanatory power when the system moves from a heat-led to a cooling-led operating regime.

\subsubsection{Cross-climate planning performance}

The final manuscript should use \Cref{fig:songshan_pareto,tab:songshan_budget} to report whether the EQD front still avoids the early saturation behavior observed in Std, and whether the resulting design shifts toward a more electricity-aware configuration in a campus-like system. The key message should be framed as \emph{cross-climate robustness}, not as absolute dominance on every single operational metric.

\subsubsection{Cooling-dominated trade-off interpretation}

This subsection must explicitly discuss the trade-off that is already visible in the placeholder results: EQD is expected to reduce external electricity dependence and increase self-sufficiency, but in the cooling-dominated case this may be accompanied by a higher curtailment ratio due to PV over-provisioning. This trade-off should not be hidden; instead, it should be used to argue that EQD prioritizes electrical balance and annual autonomy, while the planning consequence in a PV-driven campus is a more visible renewable oversupply penalty.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Carnot battery ablation}
\label{sec:exp3}

Experiment 3 isolates the planning value of Carnot battery integration by comparing the EQD method with and without Carnot battery on the Songshan Lake case. This experiment is the first half of Contribution 2: it answers whether the coupled storage device itself contributes meaningful planning value when evaluated under the proposed matching index.

\subsubsection{Planning-level value of Carnot battery integration}

The final manuscript should use \Cref{fig:exp3_carnot_ablation,tab:exp3_carnot} to report whether Carnot battery integration shifts the Pareto front toward lower matching cost, lower annualized cost, or both. Beyond front movement, this subsection should quantify whether the optimal configurations allocate non-trivial Carnot battery power and energy capacities, thereby confirming that the new device is not merely inserted into the topology but is actually selected by the optimizer.

\subsubsection{Electro-thermal coupling interpretation}

The discussion of Experiment 3 should explain \emph{why} Carnot battery value is relevant to this SCI storyline: the device creates cross-carrier coupling between electrical surplus absorption, thermal recovery, and electricity re-delivery. Therefore, if EQD can identify its value, this supports the argument that the proposed matching index is sensitive to coupled storage mechanisms rather than only to conventional single-carrier resources.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Joint validation under Carnot integration}
\label{sec:exp4}

Experiment 4 completes the second contribution by comparing Std and EQD after Carnot battery integration is enabled. While Experiment 3 asks whether Carnot battery has value, Experiment 4 asks whether the choice of matching index changes how that value is recognized by the planning framework.

\subsubsection{Index discrimination under coupled storage}

The final manuscript should use \Cref{fig:exp4_carnot_joint,tab:exp4_carnot_joint} to show whether EQD still maintains a richer Pareto structure than Std in the Carnot-enabled search space. The most important question is whether EQD allocates coupled storage more effectively, yielding better system-level performance rather than merely lower raw matching scores.

\subsubsection{Closing the second contribution loop}

This subsection should be written as the reciprocal validation of the paper's second contribution. If Experiment 3 proves that Carnot battery is useful under EQD, and Experiment 4 proves that EQD recognizes its value more effectively than Std, then the methodological contribution (energy-quality-weighted matching) and the technological contribution (Carnot battery integration) reinforce each other rather than sit as two parallel but weakly connected claims.

\subsection{Integrated discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsubsection{Carnot exergy factors as planning-stage energy quality coefficients}

The final discussion should retain the central thermodynamic message of this paper: the small magnitudes of $\lambda_h$ and $\lambda_c$ relative to $\lambda_e = 1.0$ are not a defect but a direct reflection of the second law. The job of the discussion is to connect this thermodynamic asymmetry to planning behavior: if electrical mismatch carries substantially higher work-potential loss than thermal mismatch, then a planning-stage matching index should indeed guide the optimizer toward stronger electrical balance and better grid interaction.

\subsubsection{Matching index as a proxy objective for operational quality}

The 8760-hour post-analysis should be used here to argue that EQD is not the final engineering KPI, but a proxy objective with better optimization properties than SSR, Pearson, or Std. The discussion should explain that self-sufficiency, peak demand, and curtailment are the \emph{evaluation} indicators, while EQD is the \emph{optimization} indicator that makes those outcomes reachable in a stable bi-objective search process.

\subsubsection{Carnot battery as a validator of cross-carrier sensitivity}

The Carnot battery discussion should not drift into a standalone technology review. Its role in this paper is more specific: it is the coupled-storage testbed that reveals whether the proposed index truly captures cross-carrier planning value. This keeps the second contribution aligned with the paper's main methodological storyline.

\subsubsection{Cross-climate trade-off and result framing}

The Songshan Lake results must be framed carefully. The target claim is not that EQD dominates Std on every reported metric, but that it produces a more meaningful planning trade-off: lower annual external electricity dependence and higher self-sufficiency, sometimes at the cost of higher curtailment in PV-dominated cooling-led systems. Making this trade-off explicit will strengthen the credibility of the paper.

\subsection{Limitations and future work}

Several limitations should remain explicit in the final manuscript. First, the current planning framework still relies on typical-day decomposition, so inter-day storage behavior is only partially recovered by the post-optimization full-year validation. Second, the current SCI paper should not overclaim a full uncertainty-aware operational framework; that remains the scope of the master's thesis operation layer. Third, once the server full runs are complete, the paper should still acknowledge that out-of-sample weather, load growth, and forecasting uncertainty are future directions rather than already solved parts of the current contribution.
